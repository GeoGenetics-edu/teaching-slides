From your questions

Why do Illumina reads lose quality?

Three sources of error that affect downstream annotation.

3′ quality drop
Later sequencing cycles accumulate phasing and signal decay. Quality drops toward the 3′ end of every read.
R2 is worse than R1
R2 is sequenced after R1 and additional chemistry steps. By the time R2 starts, clusters are noisier.
Adapter read-through
When the insert is shorter than the read length, the sequencer reads into the adapter. These artificial bases must be trimmed.
Per-base quality across sequencing cycles
Key idea: Quality issues at the read level propagate into gene prediction and annotation downstream.

Bridge

MAG quality controls interpretation

The quality of a MAG determines how much you can trust its annotations.

High quality
Most genes present, low contamination, strong annotations. Confident biological interpretation.
Incomplete
Missing genes, pathways, markers. Absence of annotation does not equal absence of biology.
Contaminated
False genes from other organisms. Mixed taxonomy signals, conflicting functions.

Act 1

Taxonomy

Who is this MAG related to?

Taxonomy

MAG taxonomy is genome-based

Not every MAG has a 16S gene, but every MAG has many conserved marker genes.

Key idea: Genome-based taxonomy uses many conserved marker genes, allowing classification even when the 16S gene is missing.
Taxonomy

GTDB: a genome-based taxonomy

A standardized framework that classifies organisms using genome phylogeny, not just phenotype.

Phylogenetic framework
Every genome is placed on a tree built from 120 bacterial (or 53 archaeal) concatenated marker proteins.
RED normalization
Ranks are set by Relative Evolutionary Divergence: RED = 0 at the root, RED = 1 at tips, internal nodes interpolated. Each rank spans a fixed RED band (median ± 0.1).
Standardized naming
Consistent naming across all lineages. Polyphyletic taxa are split (e.g. Bacteroides_A). Placeholder names (UBA, sp0) for genome-only lineages.
GTDB tree with standardized ranks
Key idea: GTDB is not just a list of names. It is a genome-based taxonomic framework.
Taxonomy

GTDB-Tk places MAGs into the GTDB framework

A three-step process: find markers, place on tree, assign lineage.

1. Find marker genes
Identify and extract the 120 bacterial (or 53 archaeal) marker proteins from the MAG using profile HMMs.
2. Place on reference tree
Concatenate markers and insert the MAG into the GTDB reference tree using pplacer or EPA-ng.
3. Assign lineage
Based on the placement position, assign domain through species. Compute average nucleotide identity (ANI) to the closest reference genome.
Step 1: use → to step through
Key idea: GTDB-Tk automates genome-based taxonomy: find markers, place on tree, assign ranks.
Taxonomy

Taxonomic labels have ranks and caveats

How to read a GTDB lineage string and recognize placeholder names.

d__Bacteria; p__Bacteroidota; c__Bacteroidia; o__Bacteroidales; f__Bacteroidaceae; g__Bacteroides; s__Bacteroides_A sp003456789
Rank prefixes
d__ domain, p__ phylum, c__ class, o__ order, f__ family, g__ genus, s__ species
Placeholder names
g__UBA1234 = genome-only genus
s__sp000000000 = unnamed species
Bacteroides_A = suffixed to resolve polyphyly
Key idea: Placeholder names mean the lineage is known from genomes but not formally described.
Taxonomy

Taxonomy is only as good as the MAG and the references

Four common situations that reduce confidence in a taxonomic assignment.

Missing markers
Few marker genes found in the MAG. Placement on the tree is uncertain. Confidence at deeper ranks drops.
Contaminating markers
Markers from multiple organisms create a mixed signal. The MAG may be placed between clades or assigned to a wrong lineage.
Close strains
When multiple reference genomes are nearly identical, the species-level call may be ambiguous. ANI values near the 95% threshold are borderline.
Novel lineage
The MAG has no close reference in GTDB. Assigned a placeholder name. Classification to family or order only.
Key idea: Taxonomy should always be interpreted together with MAG quality.

Act 2

From genes to functions

Before annotation, we need predicted proteins

Gene prediction

Gene prediction finds likely protein-coding regions

Prodigal scans each contig for open reading frames with coding-like patterns.

Long ORFs
Real genes tend to have long open reading frames. Short random ORFs are filtered out by length and coding potential.
Coding patterns
Codon usage, GC frame bias, and hexamer frequencies distinguish real genes from random sequence.
Start site signals
Ribosome binding sites (Shine-Dalgarno) and start codons (ATG, GTG, TTG) help identify the correct translation start.
MAG.fna → genes.gff + proteins.faa
Contig with predicted ORFs in reading frames
Key idea: Prodigal predicts genes by identifying ORFs with coding-like patterns and plausible start sites.
Gene prediction

MAGs complicate gene prediction

Fragmented assemblies and contamination create systematic prediction errors.

Contig edge cuts gene
A gene at the end of a contig is truncated. Prodigal marks it as partial, but the protein product is incomplete.
Short contig, weak context
On very short contigs, Prodigal has too little sequence to learn coding patterns. Gene calls are less reliable.
Contamination
Genes from another organism are predicted correctly, but they do not belong to this MAG. They add noise to annotation.
Frameshift or error
A sequencing error or assembly misjoining shifts the reading frame. One real gene becomes two broken ORFs.
Key idea: All downstream annotation starts from the predicted proteins. Errors here propagate.

Annotation logic

Some tools search against family models

Pairwise search matches sequences. Profile HMMs match families.

Pairwise (BLAST-like)
Compare your protein to each known sequence individually. Fast, good for close homologs. Misses remote relatives.
Profile HMM (HMMER)
Compare your protein to a statistical model of a whole family. Each position has probabilities for every amino acid. Detects remote homologs.
Where it appears
Pfam, dbCAN, GTDB-Tk markers, biosynthetic domain detection all use profile-HMM-based searches.
From alignment to profile HMM to query matching
Key idea: Profile HMMs capture the variation pattern of a whole family, not just one sequence.

Databases and interpretation

Different databases answer different biological questions

Functional databases

No database answers everything

Choose the database according to the biological question you are asking.

Question

KEGG orthology (KOfam, KO modules)
Question
What domain does this protein contain?
Pfam (protein domain families)
Question
Can it degrade carbohydrates?
CAZy / dbCAN (carbohydrate-active enzymes)
Question
Can it produce specialized metabolites?
antiSMASH / MIBiG (biosynthetic gene clusters)
Question
What broad ortholog group does this protein belong to?
eggNOG / COG (clusters of orthologous groups)
Key idea: Choose the database according to the biological question.
Functional databases

KEGG links genes to metabolism

Each gene is assigned a KO number that maps to metabolic pathways and modules.

Simplified pathway: present vs missing enzymes
Why might a gene be missing?
1. Truly absent from the organism
2. Present but too divergent to match
3. Gene is on a missing contig
4. Gene was not predicted (edge, frameshift)
5. Non-homologous replacement enzyme
Key idea: Missing annotation is not always missing biology. The gene may be absent, fragmented, divergent, or replaced.
Functional databases

Pfam identifies protein domains

Proteins are modular. Pfam detects conserved functional units within a protein sequence.

Proteins with colored domain architectures
Key idea: Proteins are modular. The same domain can appear in many proteins, and one protein can have several domains.
Functional databases

CAZymes reveal carbohydrate metabolism

Carbohydrate-active enzymes are classified into families that predict substrate specificity.

Key idea: CAZyme profiles suggest what substrates a MAG might use, but families can be broad.

Functional databases

BGCs are vulnerable to fragmentation

Assembly breaks often cut through gene clusters, reducing prediction confidence.

Complete BGC
Entire cluster on one contig, all domains present. Strongest evidence for biosynthetic potential.
BGC on contig edge
Cluster is cut at one or both contig edges. Some genes may be missing. Classification is less certain.
BGC split across gap
Parts of the cluster may be on different contigs or entirely missing. Cannot confidently reconstruct the product.
Strong candidate
NRPS cluster, 45 kb, complete on contig, 78% similarity to known BGC in MIBiG. All core domains present.
Weak candidate
Terpene cluster, 8 kb, on contig edge, no MIBiG match, single biosynthetic domain detected. Could be a false positive.
Key idea: When reading BGC predictions, ask: complete? On contig edge? Resembles known cluster?

Interpretation

Report evidence and uncertainty

Reproducible characterization requires transparency about tools, versions, and caveats.

Report
Tool names and versions (GTDB-Tk v2.x, Prodigal v2.6)
Database versions (GTDB r220, Pfam 36.0, KEGG release date)
MAG quality (completeness, contamination, MIMAG tier)
E-value thresholds and filtering criteria
Caveats (incomplete pathways, edge-cut BGCs, placeholder taxa)
Avoid
Claiming function without reporting quality
Interpreting absence of annotation as absence of biology
Using species-level taxonomy from a 60% complete MAG
Treating annotation as experimental proof of activity
Omitting database version or thresholds
Key idea: Reproducible characterization requires reporting tools, database versions, quality, and caveats.
