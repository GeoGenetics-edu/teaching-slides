THE JOURNEY

What you will learn today

We start with millions of raw reads from a mixed microbial community and end with individual draft genomes.

Act 1: Clean the data
Sequencers produce millions of short reads, but not all of them are usable. You will learn how to spot low-quality bases, adapter contamination, and other artifacts, and how trimming tools fix them before any analysis begins.
Act 2: Assemble the puzzle
Short reads overlap, and assemblers exploit that to reconstruct longer contiguous sequences (contigs). You will learn how de Bruijn graphs turn overlaps into assemblies, what makes metagenome assembly hard, and how to judge whether an assembly is any good.
Act 3: Recover genomes
Contigs come out unlabelled. Binning groups them back into individual organisms using coverage patterns and DNA composition. You will learn how modern binners work, what separates a good bin from a bad one, and how to report quality using community standards.

Preprocessing

Anatomy of a sequencing read

What a raw read actually contains, not just your DNA of interest.

1. Library construction
DNA is fragmented, then adapters, indices and sequencing primers are ligated to both ends. Most of the construct is not your sample DNA.
2. Paired-end sequencing
The sequencer reads from primer 1 (R1, forward) and primer 2 (R2, reverse). Two reads per fragment, reading inward toward each other.
3. Short insert problem
If the DNA fragment is shorter than the read length, the sequencer reads past the insert and into the opposite adapter. These bases are artificial.
4. Long insert = clean read
When the insert is longer than the read length, the read contains only biological sequence. No adapter read-through, but quality trimming may still be needed.
Library construct: use → to build up
Adapters are artificial. They’re added during library prep; they are not part of your sample’s genome.
Quality drops at the 3′ end. Signal decays as the sequencing run progresses, so errors accumulate toward the end of each read.
Short inserts = adapter read-through. When the DNA fragment is shorter than the read length, the sequencer reads into the adapter on the other side.

Why quality control matters

Every error left in the reads becomes a compounding problem downstream.

Raw input
Mixed reads with quality issues, adapters, and host contamination
Trim and clip
Remove low-quality tails and adapter sequences (fastp / cutadapt)
Length filter
Discard reads shorter than threshold after trimming (e.g. <50 bp)
Remove host
Map to host reference genome, discard aligned reads (Bowtie2)
Clean reads
Only quality-checked microbial reads remain, ready for assembly
Read quality landscape
Stepwise cleaning. Trim low-quality tails, remove adapters, filter short reads, then subtract host. Order matters.
Every read counts. One contaminated read in a bin can break a contig or introduce a false gene call. QC protects everything downstream.
Host DNA dominates. In human-associated samples, >90% of reads can come from the host. Removing them is critical for assembly.

How trimming works

Three complementary techniques to find and remove non-biological or low-quality sequence.

PE overlap detection
If insert < 2 × read length, R1 and R2 overlap. If the insert is shorter than a single read, sequencing continues into the adapter—those adapter bases must be trimmed.
5'─biological─adapter─3'
3'─adapter─biological─5'
         └──overlap──┘
Sequence matching
Slide a known adapter sequence along the read and check for alignment. Seed-and-extend: find short exact matches, then extend with mismatches allowed.
Illumina universal:
AGATCGGAAGAGC...
Sliding window
A window of w bases scans from the 3′ end. When the window mean drops below a quality threshold, everything downstream is cut.
Typical: w = 4, threshold Q ≥ 20
Q = −10 log10(Perror)
Use → to step through methods
Raw reads with quality issues
Shorter but reliable. Trimmed reads assemble better. One error base can split a de Bruijn graph node.
Adapters create false k-mers. Even a few adapter bases produce artificial edges in the assembly graph.
Don't over-trim. Aggressive quality cutoffs (Q30+) can discard >30% of data with little assembly benefit.
All three methods complement each other. PE overlap handles short inserts; pattern matching catches single-end adapters; quality trimming removes degraded tails.
Tools like fastp combine all three techniques in a single pass

Assembly

From clean reads to contigs

Assembly

Why assemble?

Short reads alone cannot answer most biological questions. Assembly reconstructs longer sequences.

The problem with short reads
A 150 bp read is too short to identify a gene (~1,000 bp), let alone a genome (~3 Mbp). We need to stitch reads back into longer contiguous sequences (contigs).
Overlap → extend → consensus
Assembly exploits the fact that reads from the same region overlap. By chaining overlaps, we reconstruct sequences far longer than any single read.
What contigs give us
Full gene sequences for annotation, longer markers for taxonomy, coverage depth for abundance, and the raw material for binning into draft genomes.
Short reads: use → to see assembly
Reads are fragments. Sequencing shatters genomes into millions of pieces. Assembly puts the puzzle back together.
Longer = more informative. A 50 kb contig carries gene context, operon structure, and synteny that a 150 bp read cannot.
Contigs, not reads, feed binning. Draft genomes (MAGs) are built from contigs grouped by composition and coverage.
Assembly

De Bruijn graph assembly

The algorithm behind MEGAHIT, SPAdes, and metaSPAdes.

1. Extract k-mers → edges
Slide a window of k bases across the read. Each k-mer becomes a graph edge connecting its prefix and suffix (k−1)-mers as nodes.
2. Build the graph
Nodes are (k−1)-mers. Consecutive k-mers share a node: the suffix of one is the prefix of the next.
3. Eulerian path
Follow an Eulerian path through the graph. Each traversed edge (k-mer) adds one base to the contig.
Use → to step through
DNA sequence
k-mers, not reads. De Bruijn assemblers work on fixed-length substrings, making them memory-efficient for large datasets.
Branches = ambiguity. Repeats and strain variants create forks in the graph, forcing the assembler to break contigs.
k matters. Small k → more connections but more tangles. Large k → cleaner graph but gaps in low-coverage regions. Tools like MEGAHIT use multiple k values.
metaSPAdes / MEGAHIT: purpose-built metagenome assemblers
Assembly

Why metagenome assembly is harder

Single-genome assemblers assume uniform coverage from one organism. Metagenomes break every assumption.

1. Uneven coverage
Dominant species at 1000×, rare at 2×. Low-coverage species fragment or vanish entirely.
2. Inter-species repeats
Conserved genes (16S, housekeeping) create cross-links in the graph, forcing contig breaks.
3. Strain collapsing
>95% ANI means most k-mers are identical, so strains merge into one consensus. Recoverable via read mapping (DESMAN, InStrain).
4. Chimeric contigs
Wrong branch at a fork → contig mixes two genomes. Poisons binning and annotation.
Coverage disparity: use → to step through
Deeper is not always better. High coverage of dominant species can drown out signal from rare organisms.
Strain resolution is the frontier. Most assemblers collapse strains into a single consensus, losing real biological diversity.
Chimeras are silent errors. They look like valid contigs but carry mixed signals that propagate through binning and annotation.
Assembly

MEGAHIT: assembly at scale

How do you build a de Bruijn graph from billions of reads without running out of memory?

1. The memory problem
Standard dBG stores every k-mer in a hash table, requiring hundreds of GB for metagenomes.
2. Encode: the SdBG trick
Sort edges, keep only the last character (W) + a boundary bit (F). ~3 bits per edge.
3. Decode: graph recovery
Compressed arrays and bitvectors encode the graph far more compactly. No hash table needed.
4. Multi-k strategy
Iterate from small k (sensitivity) to large k (specificity), merging at each round.
Memory problem: use → to step through
Memory is the bottleneck. Standard dBG can’t handle complex metagenomes on commodity hardware.
Succinct = same info, less space. W + F arrays encode the full graph in ~3 bits per edge.
Multi-k rescues rare species. Small k captures low-coverage organisms that a single large k would miss.
Assembly

Evaluating your assembly

How do you know if your assembly is good? Key statistics and what they mean.

1. N50 explained
The contig length at which 50% of the total assembly is covered by contigs of that length or longer.
2. Key statistics
N50, L50, total length, # contigs, largest contig, GC content.
3. Comparing assemblies
No single metric tells the whole story; always check multiple indicators.
N50 explained: use → to step through
N50 measures contiguity, not correctness. A chimeric assembly can have a misleadingly high N50.
Compare, don’t judge in isolation. Always evaluate metrics relative to the expected genome sizes in your sample.

Binning

Binning

From contigs to genomes

Assembly gives us thousands of contigs. Binning groups them into draft genomes (MAGs).

1. The problem
Assembly mixes contigs from many organisms into one pile. Which contig belongs where?
2. Binning
Group contigs by organism using biological signals. Each group becomes a draft genome.
3. MAGs
Metagenome-Assembled Genomes: unlocking metabolism, phylogeny, and novel taxa discovery.
Mixed contigs: use → to step through
Contigs ≠ genomes. Assembly reconstructs sequences; binning groups them into biological units.
MAGs open the door to studying uncultured organisms’ metabolism, ecology, and evolution.
Binning

Two signals for binning

How do we know which contigs belong together? Two complementary biological signals.

1. Coverage depth
How many reads map to a contig? More reads = higher coverage = the organism is more abundant.
2. Coverage covariance
Same organism → same abundance pattern across samples. Contigs that co-vary belong together.
3. Tetranucleotide frequency
Count every 4-letter DNA word in a contig. Each genome has a characteristic frequency pattern, like a fingerprint.
4. Combined power
Neither signal is perfect alone. Together they resolve ambiguous cases. This is the basis of all modern binners.
What is coverage depth?
Coverage = abundance signal. More reads mapping = more abundant organism in that sample.
TNF = composition signal. Each genome has a unique 4-mer frequency fingerprint, independent of abundance.
Binning

How a binner works

MetaBAT 2, a widely-used binner that combines both signals to cluster contigs into bins.

1. Measure signals
For each contig, compute its coverage profile and tetranucleotide frequencies.
2. Compute distances
How similar are two contigs? Combine coverage and TNF into one distance score.
3. Cluster
Group nearby contigs together. Each cluster becomes a bin (draft genome).
4. Output MAGs
Each bin is a candidate MAG. Short or ambiguous contigs may remain unbinned.
Step 1: measure signals. Use → to step through
MetaBAT 2 is the workhorse: fast, parameter-free, and widely used as a baseline binner.
More samples = better binning. Each additional sample adds another dimension for separating organisms.
Binning

Beyond hand-crafted distances

Newer binners use deep learning to discover what makes contigs similar, instead of defining it by hand.

1. Classic approach
Human-designed distance formulas. Works well but limited by what we think to measure.
2. Learned representations
Neural networks learn to map contigs into a space where similar genomes cluster naturally.
3. The landscape
From CONCOCT (2014) to COMEBin (2024). A young, fast-moving field with each generation improving.
Classic binning: use → to step through
Deep learning learns what to measure, instead of relying on pre-defined distance formulas.
The field is moving fast. Newer methods consistently improve, especially for complex or low-abundance organisms.
Binning

Quality assessment & MIMAG

Not all bins are good. How do we know if a MAG is reliable?

1. Marker genes
Every genome has ~100 genes expected exactly once. CheckM2 uses ML models trained on marker-gene profiles to estimate quality.
2. Completeness
How many markers are present? Missing markers = missing parts of the genome.
3. Contamination
Duplicated markers = DNA from another organism sneaked in. Must be low.
4. MIMAG tiers
Community standards: high (≥90%/≤5% plus 23S, 16S, 5S rRNA & ≥18 tRNA), medium (≥50%/<10%), low, or discard.
Marker genes: use → to step through
CheckM2 is widely used for estimating MAG completeness and contamination via machine-learning models trained on marker-gene profiles.
MIMAG tiers (high ≥90/≤5 + rRNA/tRNA; medium ≥50/<10) ensure reproducible quality reporting across studies.

Summary

Preprocessing. Quality control, adapter trimming, and host removal protect every downstream step. Errors left in reads compound through assembly and binning.
Assembly. De Bruijn graph assemblers like MEGAHIT reconstruct contigs from k-mers. Metagenomes add challenges: uneven coverage, inter-species repeats, and strain collapsing.
Binning. Coverage covariance and tetranucleotide frequency group contigs into MAGs. MIMAG standards ensure reproducible quality assessment.
